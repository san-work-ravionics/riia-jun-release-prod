Concepts
Investment philosophy and how professional firms approach markets
Investment Philosophies ▼

Investing is not a single strategy but a spectrum of philosophies, each built on a different theory of where value comes from and how price and value eventually converge. Value investors, in the tradition of Benjamin Graham and Warren Buffett, seek assets trading below their intrinsic worth and hold patiently for the market to correct the mispricing. Growth investors are willing to pay premium valuations today in exchange for the compounding power of above-average earnings expansion tomorrow.

Momentum investors take a different view entirely — they follow price itself, buying strength and selling weakness on the premise that trends persist longer than intuition suggests. Quantitative investing elevates any of these philosophies into a rules-based system, using data and models to generate signals free from emotional interference. What unifies the best practitioners across all schools is a shared commitment to risk-adjusted thinking: the goal is never maximum return in isolation, but the highest possible return per unit of risk accepted.

Why Retail Investors Lose Money ▼

Most retail investors do not lose because markets are unfair — they lose because human psychology is systematically mismatched with what markets demand. At emotional extremes, fear and greed take over: investors buy enthusiastically near peaks and sell in panic near troughs, locking in the exact pattern that destroys wealth over time. Overtrading compounds the problem, as each unnecessary transaction extracts a small cost in commissions, taxes, and slippage.

Recency bias keeps retail investors perpetually a step behind: treating whatever happened recently as representative of what will happen next. Without a systematic edge, trades entered on headlines or emotion compete directly against institutional algorithms. Position sizing is rarely taught yet equally important — risking too large a fraction on any single trade means one bad outcome can erase months of discipline. And the most universal failure is simply having no exit plan.

How Professional Investment Firms Invest ▼

In a professionally run investment firm, highly sophisticated quantitative finance tools and Risk / Reward processes are used by smart professionals. They use these to evaluate various investment opportunities, calculate the Risk / Reward and, as appropriate, hedge them with appropriate derivatives product(s).

CRISP-DM Phase 1
Business Understanding

RIIA stands for Risk Informed Investment Approach. This translates into the goal of ensuring the instrument or portfolio investment stays within a certain return range and the drawdown on the portfolio remains within acceptable limits. In the investment domain, the Sharpe ratio indicates how returns compare to the standard deviation of returns on the instrument or portfolio based on historical data. We therefore adopt Sharpe ratio and maximum drawdown as our core business goals.

RITA operationalises these goals by training a reinforcement learning agent that makes daily capital allocation decisions in ASML stock, targeting a Sharpe ratio above 1.0 and a maximum drawdown below 10%. The investment decision is simplified to three discrete allocations — hold cash (0%), invest half (50%), or fully invest (100%) — so the model focuses entirely on timing entry and exit.

Success is defined as consistent out-performance versus a passive buy-and-hold benchmark on a fully held-out backtest period the model never saw during training.

CRISP-DM Phase 2
Data Understanding

The ASML dataset covers daily OHLCV price history from 2019 through mid-2025 — approximately 1,500 trading sessions spanning bull runs, corrections, and recovery cycles, making it rich in both trending and mean-reverting regimes.

The publicly available data has 6 features: Date, Open, Close, High, Low and Volume. This is also called OHLCV data for instruments. From this data, eleven technical indicators are derived: RSI(14) for momentum extremes, MACD(12/26/9) and its signal line for trend momentum, Bollinger Band %B for price position relative to volatility bands, 20-day rolling volatility as a regime indicator, and daily log returns as the primary risk signal.

K-Means clustering with k=3 on the full feature matrix reveals three distinct market regimes — bullish trending, sideways consolidation, and bearish stress — each characterised by different RSI and volatility profiles that require different allocation strategies. The feature correlation matrix confirms that RSI and MACD carry complementary but non-redundant momentum signals, while 20-day volatility is largely orthogonal to both, making all three dimensions genuinely informative for the DDQN state vector.

CRISP-DM Phase 3
Data Preparation

Since the raw OHLCV dataset is already well-formed with negligible missing values and no duplicates, this phase focuses on feature engineering and structuring data for the model. The dataset start date must be set earlier than the intended modeling start so that look-back indicators such as MACD(12/26/9) have sufficient warm-up rows — starting too late pushes missing-value rates above 5%.

The correlation matrix reveals redundancy among MACD components and Stochastic oscillators; one representative per group is selected to keep the state vector non-redundant while retaining MACD as a trend-crossing signal and raw Close for portfolio tracking. All selected features are z-score normalised using parameters computed solely on the training window, then applied forward to validation and backtest windows to simulate the live inference setting. The final DDQN state vector combines non-redundant technical signals with normalised price data — not to predict Close, but to provide directional and momentum signals that guide the agent's discrete buy/hold/sell decisions.

4
CRISP-DM Phase 4
Modeling

The model uses a Double Deep Q-Network (DDQN), a reinforcement learning algorithm implemented with Stable-Baselines3. It learns from 11 market indicators observed over a 14-day period and can choose between three actions: holding cash, investing 50% of available capital, or investing 100%. The reward function encourages profitable trades while penalising portfolio drawdowns above 5%, helping the model manage risk.

Training is performed over 100,000 timesteps using experience replay and a separate target network to improve learning stability. To ensure robust results, the model is trained using three different random seeds. The seed with the highest validation Sharpe ratio is selected for final evaluation, reducing the risk of overfitting.

  • Framework: Stable-Baselines3 DQN with Double-Q target correction.
  • State Space: 8–9 features — daily_return, rsi_14, macd, bb_pct_b, trend_score, allocation, days_remaining, atr_ratio, ema_ratio.
  • Action Space: Discrete(3) — Cash (0%), Half (50%), Full (100%) invested; the agent learns when to scale in and out.
  • Network: MlpPolicy with two hidden layers of 256 units; replay buffer holds 100,000 transitions for stable off-policy learning.
  • Seed Search: train_best_of_n runs N seeds, validates each on a held-out period, keeps the best Sharpe winner.
  • Persistence: The winning model is serialised as a .zip via SB3's model.save() and reloaded for inference.

CRISP-DM Phase 5
Evaluation

The DDQN Reinforcement Learning model is evaluated through three separate datasets: a training period where the model learns patterns, a validation period used to select the best random seed, and a completely unseen backtest period that is reserved for final evaluation.

To reduce the risk of overfitting, the best seed is chosen using the validation Sharpe ratio rather than the backtest Sharpe ratio. Model performance is then assessed against the business requirements defined earlier in the project using four key metrics: Sharpe ratio (risk-adjusted return), maximum drawdown (largest loss from a peak), CAGR (annual growth rate), and win rate (percentage of profitable trades). Daily returns are also compared with a simple buy-and-hold investment in ASML to verify that the model generates genuine alpha rather than simply benefiting from overall market movements.

To support model interpretability, SHAP analysis is applied to explain the model's decisions. The results show that RSI(14) and the MACD line are the most influential signals driving portfolio allocation decisions. This transparency, together with the quantitative performance results, satisfies an important CRISP-DM requirement before the model can be considered for production deployment.

CRISP-DM Phase 6
Deployment

RIIA runs as a Dockerised FastAPI application on a cloud VM, secured over HTTPS with OAuth-based authentication for all dashboard endpoints. The 8-step training pipeline — data validation, feature engineering, model design, training, period configuration, backtesting, results extraction, and API hot-swap — is triggered from the Build section of this dashboard and executes asynchronously, with real-time step progress streamed back to the UI via polling.

Model weights (Stable-Baselines3 PyTorch .zip artefacts) are persisted to a Docker volume, surviving container restarts and rolling deployments without requiring retraining — a critical operational requirement given 100k-timestep training cycles. The DS Lab, RITA main dashboard, FnO options dashboard, and Claude Desktop MCP server all share the same FastAPI origin, meaning every CRISP-DM artefact — raw data stats, SHAP values, backtest results — is immediately accessible across the entire platform without data duplication.
